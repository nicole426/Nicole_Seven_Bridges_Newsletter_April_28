The New Boca Elite

By Dina Ulrich  |  May 1, 2026

You've heard the jokes. Boca is where people come to retire quietly, play canasta at the club, and catch the early-bird special. Except lately, someone forgot to tell the 42-year-old hedge fund partner who just closed on a waterfront estate in The Sanctuary. Or the quantum computing CEO opening a new U.S. headquarters at the old IBM campus. Or the Michigan broker-dealer moving its entire firm to a building on Yamato Road.

A Different Boca Is Arriving — Fast

For four decades, Boca Raton had one identity in the national imagination: a pastel-colored retirement zone where snowbirds migrated every November and the country clubs ran the social calendar. That caricature still shows up in sitcoms. It no longer shows up on my listing calls.

The buyers I meet today are 40, 44, 47. They run investment firms. They sold software companies. They lead trading desks. They are not looking for a golf course to retire to — they are looking for a base of operations that happens to sit 40 minutes from Miami International and three Brightline stops from downtown Miami. The retirees are still here. They are not leaving. But they are quietly sharing the zip code with a different generation of wealth, and the city's business and social fabric is reorganizing around that.

This is not a vibe shift. It is a documented economic one, and it matters if you own property here, plan to buy here, or run any business that depends on this market.

"Wall Street South" Stopped Being a Slogan

Since 2020, Palm Beach County has attracted more than 250 financial services firms — everything from small RIAs to brand-name hedge funds and private equity houses. The Business Development Board of Palm Beach County pegs the broader regional economy at roughly 19,000 companies employing 119,000 workers, with financial services driving an outsized share of the high-wage job growth.

Why they came is not a mystery. A 2025 study commissioned by the City of Boca Raton and conducted by the Boyd Company found that running a corporate headquarters in Boca costs roughly $7.5 million less per year than the equivalent operation in midtown Manhattan. No state income tax. Lower real-estate basis. Talent that already lives here or wants to. Combine those and you get a sustained multi-year migration of capital.

Two 2026 announcements tell the story better than any slide deck:

  • D-Wave Quantum — the world's first commercial quantum computing company — announced in January 2026 that it is relocating its corporate headquarters from Palo Alto to a 25,000-square-foot space at the Boca Raton Innovation Campus. The company plans 100 jobs at an average salary of $125,000, backed by performance-based incentives of up to $500,000 from the city and $5 million from the State of Florida. As part of the deal, Florida Atlantic University is investing $20 million to install a D-Wave Advantage2 quantum system on campus — the first university in the state to host one on site.
  • Concorde Investment Services — a broker-dealer and registered investment adviser — announced in March 2026 that it is moving its headquarters from Ann Arbor, Michigan, to 9,000 square feet at 301 Yamato Road, creating 30 jobs at an average salary of roughly $89,000.

These are not one-offs. They are data points on a trend line that has been steepening for five years.

Who the New Neighbors Actually Are

If you want to understand how this changes Boca, stop thinking in aggregates and start thinking in archetypes. Three profiles I see week in and week out:

The Private Equity or Hedge Fund Partner, 44. Relocated from New York or Greenwich. Two kids at one of the private schools. Bought in Royal Palm Yacht & Country Club or Woodfield. Works hybrid — some weeks out of a Class-A Boca office, some weeks on Brightline to a Miami meeting, some weeks on the Gulfstream out of the executive airport. Networks at the club on Sunday afternoons more than at the office on Tuesday mornings.

The Fintech or SaaS Founder, 39. Exited a startup two or three years ago. Building the next thing now. Chose BRiC because the engineers will actually show up to the office. Renting in Alina or Glass House downtown while he decides whether to buy in The Sanctuary or a new-construction build in St. Andrews. Does not own a golf bag. Has strong opinions about espresso.

The Relocating C-Suite Executive, 47. Took a job with one of the firms that expanded here from the Northeast. Brought the family in the same move. Wife wanted A-rated schools. He wanted the tax bill. They are quietly comparing St. Andrews Country Club to Broken Sound and running the numbers on initiation fees, carrying costs, and resale liquidity before deciding.

These are the buyers writing $3M to $20M checks. They are not ostentatious. They are deliberate. And they are reshaping what sells, where it sells, and at what velocity.

The City Has Been Building For Them On Purpose

The infrastructure didn't happen by accident. The Brightline station opened in December 2022, making Miami a 45-minute commute and Orlando a three-hour one. Class-A office supply is tighter than it has been in a generation. And the old IBM R&D campus — the actual birthplace of the personal computer — is being rebuilt into a live-work-play hub.

The Boca Raton Innovation Campus (BRiC) now spans 1.7 million square feet across 123 acres and houses 40-plus companies in tech, life sciences, and healthcare. In January 2026 the owners celebrated completion of a $100 million amenity program that added a wellness center, coffee concepts, an autonomous market, a dining hall, event space, two fitness studios, and a 1,100-space garage. The 2023 rezoning cleared the way for 1,243 residential units, 125,000 square feet of retail, a boutique hotel, and medical offices. Groundbreaking on the residential portion is targeted for 2026; initial delivery is expected in the 2028–2030 window.

In March 2026 a new private school — Palm Beach International Academy — opened at BRiC. It serves grades 6–12 with a hybrid model built for student-athletes, performers, and kids managing high-pressure schedules. It is not a coincidence that the school went where the tech and finance employers went.

One Boca: The Guardrail Nobody Expected

Boca's transformation is not frictionless. In January 2026, the City Council voted 4-1 to approve a 99-year public-private partnership with Terra and Frisbie Group called "One Boca" — a redevelopment of the city's downtown government campus into hundreds of residential units, a boutique hotel, retail, a new city hall, and a pedestrian promenade to the Brightline station. Proponents projected $4 billion in lease revenue over the life of the deal.

Two months later, on March 10, 2026, Boca voters rejected the plan in a landslide — 74.52% against, 25.48% in favor. Three Save Boca-aligned candidates won City Council seats in the same election. The message from the electorate was unambiguous: growth is welcome, but not at any cost, and not on public land without broad consent.

"The vote didn't say no to growth. It said no to that specific deal. The wealth migration is happening with or without One Boca."

For anyone watching the market, this matters less than the headlines suggested. The demand drivers — corporate relocations, school demand, tax structure, lifestyle — are all still pulling capital into Boca. The referendum changed the downtown skyline's 10-year plan. It did not slow the pace of high-end waterfront sales.

Old Boca Status vs. New Boca Status

The cultural shift is where this gets interesting to watch. The old Boca elite flashed it. The new one hides it. Call it the "quiet luxury" effect arriving at the Florida border. The signals are different. The networks are different. What people brag about is different.

The country clubs themselves are adapting. St. Andrews Country Club completed its $27.5 million enhancement program in late 2023 after a record sales year — and the membership is now actively contemplating another major expansion, a second transformative capital plan that would build on the momentum of the first. That kind of back-to-back investment by an established club tells you everything about who the new member is and what they expect. St. Andrews is not waiting for the next generation of buyers to arrive; it is engineering the amenity set those buyers will demand when they get here. Broken Sound, with its 1,600-plus homes across 1,000 acres, is running initiation tiers from $130,000 to $225,000 as of October 2025 — priced for working professionals, not retirees. Woodfield has leaned hard into family amenities and a nationally ranked tennis program. The message across the board: we are not the golf-then-early-dinner club your grandparents belonged to.

What This Means If You Own, Buy, or Sell Here

The shift is not abstract. It changes pricing strategy, neighborhood priorities, and negotiating leverage. Three things to internalize:

If you're selling

The buyer on your property is increasingly coming from out of state, comparing your home not to Delray or Palm Beach Gardens but to a $7 million apartment on the Upper East Side or a $5 million townhouse in Brookline. They want turnkey. They are not doing renovation projects. The return on a pre-listing refresh — new kitchen, bathrooms, landscaping, smart-home — is higher than it has been in years. Price to the out-of-state comparison set, not the local one.

If you're buying

Pay attention to lifestyle proximity, not just square footage. Walkability to downtown, Brightline access, and school district all price in now in a way they did not in 2018. Downtown-adjacent and BRiC-adjacent inventory is the appreciation story to watch. For the top of the market, The Sanctuary and Royal Palm Yacht & Country Club remain the prestige benchmarks and trade at a premium that reflects it. For new-construction luxury, St. Andrews and Boca Bridges continue to produce the bulk of turnkey estate-grade inventory.

If you're a long-term resident

Your home is worth more than it was, your taxes are probably the same or lower, and your neighbors are more likely to be a 42-year-old executive than a 72-year-old retiree. The tension is real — traffic, school capacity, service-class affordability — but the underlying economic gravity is not reversing. Participate in the civic conversation (the One Boca vote proves it matters), but don't confuse change for decline. Boca is becoming more valuable, not less.

"The new buyer wants turnkey and modern. They're pricing your home against Manhattan, not Delray. Price to that buyer, and the numbers come in higher."

The Next Chapter

Boca is not turning into Miami. It is not turning into West Palm. It is becoming something distinctly its own — a city that took the best of what it always was (safe, polished, well-located, luxury lifestyle at a discount to the Northeast) and quietly layered a new economic and social identity on top of it. A quantum computing company down the street from a golf club. A $200 billion investment firm one exit from a family-focused school. A hedge fund founder who could live anywhere — and chose here.

The retirees are still here. They built this place. They are not going anywhere. They just have a 42-year-old neighbor now, and that neighbor is reshaping the market in real time.

The next time someone cracks a joke about Boca being "God's waiting room," tell them about the $29.5 million estate Billy Joel bought in The Sanctuary in January 2024 — and the additional $14 million he spent six months later to add the house next door. Tell them about the quantum computer getting installed at FAU. Tell them about the Michigan broker-dealer that just moved its entire firm to Yamato Road.

And then tell them to pay attention, because the Boca they grew up joking about is not the Boca that's arriving.
